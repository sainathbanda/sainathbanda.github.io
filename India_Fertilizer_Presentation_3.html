03The Subsidy Architecture & Its Fiscal Risk

India's fertilizer subsidy operates through two distinct mechanisms. The MRP freeze on urea creates unlimited fiscal exposure — the government is a residual price-taker with zero protection from world price volatility. Every rupee of world price increase above ₹242/bag flows directly into government expenditure.

06The NPK Imbalance — 30 Years of Structural Distortion

India's N:P:K ratio deteriorated from 5.9:2.4:1 in 1991-92 to an extreme 12.8:5.1:1 in Kharif 2022. This is not just a soil problem — it is a policy-designed structural failure driven by artificially cheap urea and chronically under-subsidised potash, reducing the return on every rupee of subsidy spent.

Four Self-Reinforcing Feedback Loops — Why These Crises Compound and Do Not Resolve Quickly:

① Fiscal Squeeze → Higher subsidy crowds out spending on irrigation, rural infra, and agricultural R&D — reducing long-run resilience to the next crisis
② Soil Degradation → NPK imbalance worsens soil health → lower crop response → more fertilizer needed next season → more imports required → repeat
③ Farmer Distress → If MRP rises even partially, small and marginal farmers reduce application → yield loss → rural income shock → food demand collapse
④ Import Spiral → Panic buying at peak prices → India's large tender size actually pushes global prices up further → India pays even more for the same volume
