Carbon Credits in Indian Agriculture: Where We Are, What's Changing, and How to Make It Work for Farmers
March 2025CarbonPolicy· 8 min read
▾

India's carbon credit landscape is evolving rapidly. From the launch of the domestic Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) under the Energy Conservation Act to grassroots voluntary carbon projects in Maharashtra and Telangana — this is a pivotal moment for agricultural carbon markets in India.

Where We Are Today

India has committed to net-zero by 2070 and reducing emissions intensity by 45% by 2030. Agriculture contributes roughly 14% of India's GHG emissions, but also holds enormous sequestration potential through zero tillage, cover cropping, agroforestry, and better livestock management. The domestic carbon market is still nascent — the Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE) is developing the Indian Carbon Market (ICM) framework, with the first compliance sectors going live around 2026. Agriculture will likely remain in the voluntary segment near-term.

"The question is not whether Indian farmers can generate carbon credits — they can. The question is whether the system is designed to include them." — 6th International Agronomy Congress, 2025

Key Challenges for Smallholder Inclusion

The Three Core Barriers
  • MRV (Measurement, Reporting & Verification): Smallholder plots are tiny and scattered, making remote sensing and soil sampling expensive per credit generated.
  • Aggregation: Individual farmers can't transact directly — they need aggregators (FPOs, NGOs, companies) which creates principal-agent problems and erodes farmer income share.
  • Permanence & Additionality: Proving reductions are real and permanent is methodologically hard for subsistence farmers who may switch practices due to drought or price shocks.

What Researchers Need to Focus On

Based on my research on carbon credit projects in Maharashtra and Telangana (TAFSSA/CIMMYT), here are the pressing gaps: discrete choice experiments on farmer preferences for program design; cost-benefit analysis of MRV technologies at village scale; equity analysis of who gets included; and policy integration with existing schemes like PM-KUSUM and PKVY. If you're a student looking for a topic — carbon farming economics is wide open and highly fundable right now.

How I Cracked ICAR-SRF (AIR 11): My Preparation Strategy
Feb 2025Career· 6 min read
▾

A lot of students ask me how I prepared. I secured All India Rank 11 in ICAR-SRF (2020), having earlier secured AIR 16 in ICAR-JRF. Here's exactly what I did.

Understand the Pattern First

ICAR-SRF for Agricultural Economics is a 2-hour objective exam covering: Production Economics, Farm Management, Agricultural Finance & Cooperation, Marketing, Price Analysis, NRE, Trade Policy, and Research Methodology. Map every topic to the official syllabus before studying.

Core Study Material I Used
  • AgriMoon e-courses — free, comprehensive. I read every Agricultural Economics module.
  • ICAR e-Courses (IASRI) — especially Research Methodology and Statistics
  • Previous Year Papers — the single most important resource. Do at least 5 years under timed conditions.
  • Penson, Capps & Rosson — production economics foundations
  • Johl & Kapur — Farm Business Management

Daily Routine (Last 3 Months)

  • Morning (2 hrs): New topic from notes/e-courses
  • Afternoon (1 hr): Previous year questions on that topic
  • Evening (1 hr): Revision of previous day's material
  • Weekend: Full mock test + analysis of mistakes
Focus on understanding concepts, not memorising. ICAR exams test application, not recall. Consistency over intensity — 4 focused hours beats 10 scattered ones.

Feel free to reach out if you have specific questions about preparation — happy to help.

Bibliometric Analysis Using VOSviewer: A Step-by-Step Guide
Jan 2025Methods· 10 min read
▾

Bibliometric analysis is increasingly popular in agricultural economics. Our blockchain in agri-food paper (published in JADEE, IF 2.3) used this method. Here's how to do it from scratch.

Step 1 — Build Your Search Query

Go to Web of Science or Scopus. Build a Boolean query — e.g. for carbon farming: ("carbon credit*" OR "carbon farming") AND ("agriculture" OR "smallholder"). Export all records as plain text (.txt for WoS) or .csv for Scopus — select "Full Record and Cited References".

Step 2 — Import into VOSviewer

  • Open VOSviewer → "Create a map based on bibliographic data"
  • Select your data source → upload your file
  • Choose analysis: Co-authorship, Co-citation, or Keyword Co-occurrence (most common)
  • Set minimum keyword occurrence to 5 for datasets of 200-500 papers
Reading the Map
  • Clusters (colours): Research themes — keywords that appear together
  • Node size: Frequency — bigger = more common
  • Line thickness: Strength of co-occurrence between two keywords
  • Distance: Closer = more strongly related

Step 3 — Deeper Analysis with bibliometrix in R

VOSviewer is great for visualisation but bibliometrix in R gives you annual publication trends, top authors, top journals, h-index of the field, and Bradford's Law. Install with install.packages("bibliometrix") and run biblioshiny() for an interactive dashboard. The combination of both tools covers a complete bibliometric paper.

Beyond Milk: Valuing the Full Ecosystem Services of the Murrah Buffalo
Nov 2024Livestock· 9 min read
▾

When we value a Murrah buffalo, we almost always look at milk yield and milk price alone. But this misses the picture dramatically. A Murrah buffalo is a multi-dimensional asset providing provisioning, regulating, and cultural ecosystem services.

Full Bundle of Ecosystem Services
  • Provisioning: Milk, meat (end of life), hide, bones
  • Regulating: Dung for biogas and organic fertiliser, carbon sequestration through soil health improvement
  • Cultural: Religious significance, social status, cultural identity in rural communities
  • Supporting: Nutrient cycling, soil structure improvement

Why This Matters for Policy

Valuing only milk systematically undervalues the Murrah buffalo and leads to under-investment in conservation and welfare. Insurance schemes, credit products, and breed improvement programs are all calibrated to milk value alone. A comprehensive valuation framework changes this calculus.

Methodological Approach

My ongoing research uses a multi-dimensional framework combining: market price method for milk and dung; benefit transfer for carbon sequestration; and contingent valuation for cultural services. Some services (cultural, spiritual) resist full monetisation — but they still deserve documentation even if not fully priced.

True economic valuation of livestock must go beyond the farmgate price. The Murrah's contribution to rural livelihoods is far greater than any single metric captures.

How U.S. Tariffs Affect Indian Farmers: Insights from a CGE Model
Oct 2024Policy· 8 min read
▾

When the U.S. announces reciprocal tariffs, most debate focuses on industry and IT. But what does it mean for a paddy farmer in Bihar or a cotton grower in Telangana? Our policy paper (ICAR-RCER & IFPRI, 2025) used a Computable General Equilibrium (CGE) model to find out.

What is a CGE Model?

A CGE model represents the entire economy as interconnected markets — production, consumption, trade, and factor markets — that simultaneously clear at equilibrium prices. Shock one part (a 26% tariff) and the model calculates ripple effects across all sectors, all income groups, all regions.

Key Findings
  • Agriculture faces indirect but significant effects — labour moves from export-affected industries into agriculture, depressing wages
  • Export-oriented commodities like marine products, spices, and rice face direct demand reduction
  • Rural households in bottom income quintiles are disproportionately affected through lower real wages
  • Bigger impact is on farm income, not food prices

CGE is the right tool when the shock is economy-wide and you care about distributional consequences. The limitation: CGE models require strong assumptions about substitution elasticities — always be transparent about these. The full paper is available through ICAR-RCER, Patna — write to me for a copy.

How to Write Your First Research Paper: A Guide for M.Sc. Students
Aug 2024MethodsCareer· 11 min read
▾

Nobody teaches you how to write a research paper — you're expected to figure it out. Here's the framework I wish I'd had as an M.Sc. student.

Start with a Research Question, Not Data

The most common mistake: starting with data instead of a question. A good research question is specific, answerable with available data, and contributes something new — even if small. Not "what affects crop yield" but "does access to irrigation affect wheat yield among marginal farmers in X district."

The IMRaD Structure
  • Introduction: Why does this matter? What gap are you filling?
  • Methods: How did you collect data? What model and why?
  • Results: What did you find? Tables, figures — facts only, no interpretation.
  • Discussion: What do results mean? Compare to literature. Limitations?
  • Conclusion: So what? What should policymakers do?

Write Methods First, Introduction Last

Counterintuitively, write: Methods → Results → Discussion → Conclusion → Introduction → Abstract. The introduction is easiest once you know exactly what you found.

Responding to Reviewers

Use a response table: reviewer comment | your response | changes made. Never argue aggressively — thank them, address the concern, explain your reasoning calmly.

A rejection is not a failure — it is feedback. Almost every paper that eventually gets published was rejected at least once first.
